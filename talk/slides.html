Designing a Student-Centered Open Textbook

JMM 2026, Washington DC

Geoff Cox, Blain Patterson
Applied Mathematics @ Virginia Military Institute

December 29, 2025

Abstract

Ordinary differential equations (ODEs) form a gateway course for many STEM majors, yet students often encounter steep conceptual and technical barriers when transitioning from calculus to differential equations. This talk presents a case study in rethinking ODE pedagogy through the development of an open-access textbook that blends student-centered narrative, scaffolded checkpoints, interactive technology, and structured workflows.

The projectâ€™s guiding question is: How can textbook design itself function as an innovation in teaching and learning? Each chapter is built around conversational explanations, step-by-step workflow and reading checkpoints that emphasize concepts and procedure. Interactive visualizations add a dynamic perspective to simulate a classroom experience.

By framing the textbook not only as a resource but as a pedagogical experiment, this work highlights the role of curriculum design in shaping student experiences of ODEs and opens space for dialogue about how innovative resources can transform undergraduate mathematics education.

Why Rethink the Differential Equations Textbook?

Differential equations is a gateway course for many STEM majors, yet the materials we use are often expensive, static, and mismatched to how students actually learn today.

  • Commercial texts are polished but rigid.
  • Free notes exist, but often lack structure and support.
  • Students increasingly expect interactive, accessible, and digital-first resources.

This talk describes the design of a student-centered, interactive textbook and the tools that made it sustainable.

From Tool to Design

Textbooks are typically written, reviewed, and evaluated by experts in the field. As a result, they often prioritize disciplinary completeness over how students actually experience the material.

That tension led to our guiding design question:

What would a differential equations textbook look like if it were written entirely for students?

Part II: Textbook Design

From Design to Evaluation

The interactive features in this textbook were not added for novelty, but to address persistent challenges in how students learn differential equations.

To move beyond anecdote, we are conducting a formal study to examine how students actually engage with these features and what they perceive as beneficial or challenging.

This work is framed as a small mixed-methods pedagogical study.

Part III: Pedagogical Evaluation

The guiding research question is:

In what ways do interactive textbooks enhance studentsâ€™ understanding of differential equations concepts?

Data is collected through a student survey, and is supplemented by open-ended and reflective focus groups.

The emphasis is on student engagement, conceptual understanding, persistence, and accessibility.

What We Are Measuring

Rather than testing isolated skills, the study examines how students use the textbook while learning.

  • How students engage with low-stakes reading questions
  • Whether feedback prompts reflection and persistence
  • Which interactive elements support or hinder learning
  • How students rate the experience to traditional books

These measures are designed to connect textbook features directly to learning behaviors.

Why This Matters

Differential equations is conceptually challenging because it asks students to reason about dynamic systems using static representations.

Interactive textbooks have the potential to:

  • Make abstract behavior more concrete and visual
  • Encourage struggle through immediate feedback
  • Support persistence when students answer incorrectly
  • Improve accessibility through multimodal engagement

This study helps evaluate whether those potential benefits are realized in practice.
